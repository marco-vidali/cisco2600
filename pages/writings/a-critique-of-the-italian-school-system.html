a critique of the italian school system

home writings
Human beings love to learn. It is part of our nature. Observe, for example, a small child the moment they see snow falling for the first time: they run outside barefoot, touch it, watch it melt in their hands, taste it, throw it, and ask their mother: "Why does it fall from the sky?", "Why is it cold?", "Can I make a ball out of it?". All of this happens without any compulsion, without anyone telling them what to do. Yet there they are, freezing in the middle of the snow, eager to explore it and curious to discover its true nature.

This curiosity is not present only in two- or three-year-old children: it lasts an entire lifetime and manifests itself in adolescents, adults, and the elderly alike. Among these, students—generally teenagers—are the most curious: they teach themselves how to code from scratch, how to play the piano, how to cook, how to play soccer, how to paint... the desire to learn is inherent in them and allows them to grow in a tangible way.

We can therefore understand that "education" means precisely this: the ability to express one’s personality and passions to the fullest, drawing out what is already within each of us and fueling it by learning new things that help achieve one’s ambitions. This, then, is what an educational institution should do: bring to light that which allows one to express themselves completely and enable everyone to fulfill their potential as a person.

It is, therefore, necessary to ask whether the most influential educational body in our country—the school system, in which everyone is required to participate—genuinely aims for this goal. To best understand the functioning of this vast system, let us analyze an average day for a typical Italian high school student.

It all begins at eight o'clock in the morning: after waking up at dawn with great difficulty and reluctance, the student must prepare to face five hours of school where they are filled, like a garbage bag, with notions upon notions covering a vast range of disciplines: from mathematics to physics, through Latin and literature, across biology and chemistry, concluding with art history. What they are taught consists mainly of data, facts, information, and nothing more: the life of Leopardi, the calculation of derivatives, the structure of proteins... and this is the same school system that claims to cherish the teaching—and the subsequent denunciation—of Dickens’ "Nothing but Facts!" passage.

When the five hours are over, the student leaves school tired and bored, enthusiastic only because this torture has ended for the day: should they not, on the other hand, leave school happy to have learned something new? No: learning is reduced to a mechanical duty, an ingestion of facts disconnected from real life.

Finally, in the early afternoon, the student arrives home. After a hurried lunch and a little time dedicated to themselves—not much, to avoid being smothered by the lack of time to study—the student spends hours and hours absorbing the notions taught to them during the morning.

These notions, however, do not spark the slightest interest in the student: they are forced to study them merely to be evaluated with a pathetic number, which does not represent them in the slightest. Furthermore, all of this can cause problems such as anxiety and stress, which undoubtedly hinder their personal growth and the passing of their "golden years."

It is now evening, and our student has spent the whole day buried in books. The time has finally come, perhaps, for them to relax and dedicate time to themselves—or perhaps not: they may have to spend the rest of the day in their books, until late at night, in order to prepare for an upcoming test and earn a few hours of turbulent sleep.

Let us see, however, what happens the following day. It all begins at eight in the morning: the student, after waking up at dawn with great difficulty and reluctance, must prepare to face five hours of school where they are filled, like a garbage bag—wait, is this not exactly what happened the day before? Exactly. Our student's days are all the same, without any variety, in which they are forced to repeat from scratch everything they did the day before and will do the next, like a little machine.

And that is not all! Because this exhausting situation could lead to something even greater: the student, drained and crushed by this unbearable burden, might give up and renounce the time intended for school altogether. However, they would not dedicate this newfound free time to themselves, but rather—due to the attrition caused by school—would spend their entire days procrastinating, using social media, or doing nothing concrete. They are transformed, therefore, into an alienated individual whose only goal is to survive.

Come now! After a few years, all this effort will have been worth something to our dear student, right? Certainly! Our student has finally forgotten completely everything they had learned with joy and delight during their school years, and thanks to all the time they were able to dedicate to the development of their passions, they have become an ordinary employee, forced to obey others' orders in some random company!

Who knows who they could have become if they had enjoyed a proper education... perhaps they could have become a computer genius, a prodigy painter, or a life-saving psychologist—exactly what this utilitarian world would need, after all. But no! We do not want any of this! We do not want everyone to become the best version of themselves! We want everyone to turn into beings forced to continually perform the same insignificant job, without any possibility of growth!

Thank you, oh school, for draining the essence of everyone! And congratulations for calling this "education"!

As if that were not enough, the Italian school system offers us another absurdity connected to all this: the drastic ban on mobile phones. In fact, the Minister of Education and Merit, Giuseppe Valditara, recently issued a ministerial circular that definitively bans the use of phones during school hours, even during recess. Yet, the same school system is presented as being open to technological progress: the first objective of the PNRR (National Recovery and Resilience Plan), in fact, aims at the digitization and technological development of the country. So, can we really consider this drastic measure consistent with the goal the system claims to pursue, namely the integration of digital tools that define the era in which we live?

Indeed, it is well known that the phone can be used during school hours not as a means of learning, but as a means of entertainment and distraction. As seen before, however, most students find lessons boring, in large part because they are not the least bit interested and do not feel represented: are we sure, then, that the problem lies truly with the students and not with the way they are being educated? Are we sure that, if students received the education they deserve, they would still feel the need to distract themselves by using their phones during school hours?

This drastic measure is, therefore, a symbol of the absolute laziness of the Italian school system: it represents a method to avoid a problem that it does not want to address, preferring to deprive students of a means of information rather than seeking to improve the educational situation.

It follows, therefore, that the Italian school system is a hypocritical one: it contemplates and welcomes technological progress and digitization, but instead of integrating technologies, such as phones, into the educational process—to allow students to learn better and teach them how to use them—it decides to ban them and dump all the blame on its students, washing its hands of the matter.

Oh Italian school system, if you truly want to remain consistent with your contradictory decisions, ban all electronic devices, including tablets and computers, because you know better than your young citizens how they can be used in the same way phones would be used!

I hope, therefore, that all teachers, professors, and above all, vice-ministers and the Minister of Education and Merit—the current Giuseppe Valditara—who feel addressed by this essay, will not dismiss it with indifference, but will reflect on their choices and actions which reflect the systematic alienation and contradictory hypocrisy denounced in these pages, because they hold the lives of millions of young people in their hands.
