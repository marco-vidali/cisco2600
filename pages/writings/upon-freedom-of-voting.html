Voting is the right that allows every human being to fully define themselves as a citizen, and one of its fundamental characteristics is its libertarian nature: every individual must be free to express their political preferences autonomously and without coercion. This coercion can be of two types: physical and psychological. Physical constraints are those that commonly come to mind when thinking about violations of freedom, such as the kidnapping of an individual voter; psychological constraints, on the other hand, are less immediate and require a deeper analysis.

Psychological coercion can be defined as any preferences and opinions that have been instilled in an individual through manipulative mechanisms by a politician in order to gain greater consensus. It is precisely these limitations that most violate the libertarian nature of this right: they take root within the citizens themselves, who remain unaware of them and convinced that they are free in expressing their vote. Consequently, these limitations prevent citizens from thinking freely and truly expressing their own ideals and desires.

The most common manipulative mechanism is triggering a strong sense of representation and belonging in citizens. By positioning themselves as "just like everyone else," a politician manages to blend in with the electorate and obtain greater consensus than others solely through a purely emotional affinity, completely disregarding any political factor. This dynamic, in fact, represents the first step toward creating a bound and independent community.

These psychological mechanisms inevitably lead to another factor that heavily influences the electorate: the tendency to vote for a party simply because it is common practice in the society one belongs to. It is not uncommon for an individual to support a party or a politician merely because "that's just what people do," without really knowing why. In fact, when trying to understand the reasoning behind this choice, one never receives clear or convincing answers.

The election campaigns that perfectly exemplify these phenomena are those of Luca Zaia. Zaia strictly utilizes these psychological mechanisms to influence most of the Venetian population: if you listen to any of his speeches, you will always find a phrase, an expression, or an idiom in the Venetian dialect. In doing so, Zaia has always placed himself on the same level as the citizens, sparked a strong sense of belonging within them, and gained widespread consensus through a simple emotional connection. A sense of belonging that was strictly fueled by the grand promise—which, of course, was never fulfilled—to make Veneto an independent region.

This combination of factors has contributed enormously to consolidating Luca Zaia's figure, transforming a vote for him into an actual social norm in Veneto: during his fifteen years in office, anyone who deviated from this trend was almost looked upon with suspicion. As proof of this, one can analyze the data from the 2020 regional elections: Zaia managed to obtain around 77% of the votes, representing more than three-quarters of the Venetian population.

And let no one dare say that people vote for him because he is a "good politician," especially after seeing the lifetime debt caused by the financial disaster of the Pedemontana highway! An administration that has led to a shortage of general practitioners due to a suffocating bureaucracy that leaves entire towns without healthcare; the emergency room crisis, forced to resort to hiring foreign doctors with degrees not recognized in Italy just to cover shifts; and endless waiting lists that force Venetian citizens to pay out of pocket by turning to private healthcare!

Unfortunately, these cases do not only happen at the regional level, but also at the municipal level. Recently, the mayoral elections were held in Torre di Mosto, and among the candidates was Andrea Marchesin, a public administration official backed by the Lega party.

Andrea Marchesin decided to include a meeting with Luca Zaia in his election campaign: by exploiting the figure of the former Venetian president and all the psychological mechanisms previously used by him, he aimed to win the consensus of the local citizens (torresani), thus getting closer to the title of mayor without having to rely exclusively on the actual value of his political program. And, of course, he succeeded!

But, as expected, once he came to power, the fragility of his political program emerged, accompanied by massive contradictions. Just one day after his election, he declared through Instagram and Facebook posts that he had started urban decorum works and road signage installation. However, the following day, official documents were published by a municipal opposition list, proving that those works had already been started by the previous mayor. This fact is therefore proof of how this mayor continues to seek the approval of citizens through manipulative and bogus methods, even after rising to power.

Thus, it is easy to clearly understand how citizens' political preferences that are not based exclusively on political principles inevitably lead to the election of figures who do not contribute in any way to improving the economic, political, or social situation in Italy. Perhaps, this is why Italy does not and will never move forward. Perhaps, in Italy, citizens are not truly free to vote.
